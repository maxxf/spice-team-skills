# How to Write Like Maxx Freedman

A voice guide for the content team at Notice Media / Spice Digital. Everything in here was pattern-matched from real emails, LinkedIn posts, sales calls, and Slack messages. Not aspirational. This is how Maxx actually sounds.

Use this as your north star before publishing anything under his name or the Spice brand.

---

## The Short Version

Maxx writes like he talks: direct, specific, a little spicy. He gets to the point in the first sentence, backs it up with numbers, and closes with a clear next step. If a sentence doesn't advance the message, it gets cut. If a paragraph sounds like a committee wrote it, it gets rewritten.

The vibe is "smart friend who works in the industry and is being straight with you."

---

## How Maxx Opens

Every piece starts with **the point**. Not a warm-up. Not a thesis. The point.

**In emails**, it's almost always: name, comma or dash, then immediately why you're reading this.

Real examples:

- "Alonzo, Congrats on the growth. Excited to bring the new locations online across all three platforms."
- "Andy & Jada, reaching out because we have an invoice that is nearly a month overdue with another one due in 6 days."
- "Paul, I'm not a Deliverect expert but we have used the platform with several clients."
- "Amro, thanks for submitting the contact form earlier. I'm Maxx, founder at Spice."

**On LinkedIn**, it's a specific moment or a provocative statement. Never a thesis.

- "I just finished my first call of the day at 7am ET..."
- "I selected the wrong types of clients."

**For cold outreach**, it's three sentences. Link to the deck. Link to book a call. Done. No essay.

The rule: if your first sentence could be deleted and nothing would be lost, your second sentence is your real opener. Move it up.

## Sentence Structure

Maxx varies sentence length like a drummer varies tempo. One word. Then a longer sentence that builds and layers context. Then something short again that lands.

This is the single biggest tell between AI-written copy and Maxx-written copy. AI makes every paragraph the same length, every sentence roughly the same cadence. Maxx doesn't.

Compare:

**AI pattern:** "The delivery marketplace landscape continues to evolve rapidly. Restaurant operators face increasing commission pressures while simultaneously managing multiple platform relationships. Understanding these dynamics is essential for maintaining profitability."

**Maxx pattern:** "Commission rates are going up. Uber Eats just announced a 1.5% hike across every tier, effective March 11th. If you're running on tight margins (and who isn't), pull your P&L and do the math before it hits."

The second version has a one-liner, a detail-packed sentence, and a direct call to action with a parenthetical that shows personality. That's the rhythm.

## How He Makes Arguments

When Maxx needs to persuade someone or make a case, it follows this structure:

1. **Quick acknowledgment** of the situation (brief, not deferential)
2. **Facts** with specific numbers, dates, or examples
3. **The ask or recommendation**, stated clearly with no hedging
4. **A path forward**: "Happy to jump on a call," "Sound good?", or "Let me know how you'd like to proceed"

He does the math for the reader. Instead of "affordable per-location pricing," he writes: "At 10 locations that's $362/location for full-service management across Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Grubhub. Most operators pay more than that for a single platform."

The reframe is what makes people actually feel the value. Don't tell them it's a good deal. Show them the math and let the number speak.

---

## How He Handles Hard Conversations

Maxx doesn't soften hard messages with corporate padding. He states the problem directly, but without being hostile. The tone is "I respect you enough to be straight about this."

**Late payments:** "Reaching out because we have an invoice that is nearly a month overdue with another one due in 6 days. I want us to get current on payments this week." Then the invoice link. Then "Thank you." No guilt trip, no threats, no long explanation.

**Calling out a bad tool:** "Honestly, our internal reporting has outpaced what Loop provides. The 3-day data delay and some reliability issues have made it hard to use Loop as a primary source. I'd rather be straight with you about it than keep paying for something neither of us is getting full value from."

**Flagging ops problems:** "The two biggest blockers to growth right now are ops and menus. Until stores are running clean and the menu is optimized, we're capped on what we can do with ad spend and campaigns." Then numbered items with specific owners and deadlines.

Notice: he names the problem first, explains why briefly, and then gives a path forward. No paragraph of context before the punchline.

## Voice Registers

Different contexts get different energy levels, but the core voice stays the same.

| Context | Energy | Key Traits |
|---------|--------|------------|
| Client email | Professional-direct | Name opener, facts first, "Happy to jump on a call" |
| Cold outreach | Ultra-short | 3 sentences max, deck link, booking link, done |
| Late payment / escalation | Firm-fair | State the problem immediately, clear ask, no softening |
| Sales follow-up | Warm-specific | Reference what was discussed, propose next step |
| LinkedIn post | Story-driven | Hook with a moment, short paragraphs, end with a question or bold take |
| Slack / casual | Fragments | "kk", abbreviations, lowercase fine, personality first |
| Newsletter (Spicy Nuggets) | Informed-spicy | All lowercase (except proper nouns), attitude, short punchy paragraphs, bold key numbers |
| Proposal / formal doc | Structured-authoritative | Still Maxx, but with numbers and clear section breaks |
| Internal team | Coach mode | Direct feedback, named owners, clear deadlines |

When in doubt, ask: "Would Maxx actually say this out loud to the person?" If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like a sharp conversation over coffee, you're close.

## Signature Moves

These closers show up constantly and are part of the brand voice:

- **"Happy to jump on a call to talk through any of this!"** for substantive emails
- **"Sound good?"** after action items or proposals
- **"Let me know how you'd like to proceed"** when handing a decision back
- **"When ready, feel free to book some time to chat here"** with a cal.com link for prospects
- **"I'd rather be straight with you"** when delivering uncomfortable truths

## The Spice

The newsletter is literally called Spicy Nuggets. The brand is called Spice. Personality isn't optional.

This doesn't mean being edgy for no reason. It means:

- Having an opinion where others hedge. "The formula is simple and most brands overthink it."
- Using specific details that show you actually know the industry, not just summarizing press releases
- Self-deprecating humor when it fits. "For some reason decided I was like, I'm gonna consult with restaurants. Kind of a masochistic decision."
- The occasional sharp line that has attitude. "I selected the wrong types of clients. Big brands with big checks felt validating. They were also the biggest headaches."
- Engaging the reader directly. "What's your version of 'they ate the corn husk'?"

One moment of personality per piece minimum. That's the bar.

## The Kill List

These words and patterns are banned. If you catch yourself writing any of them, rewrite the sentence.

### Banned Phrases (hard no)

- Em dashes. Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead.
- "Genuinely" / "Straightforward" / "Here's the kicker"
- "It's not about X, it's about Y"
- "At the end of the day" / "In today's [anything]"
- "Let's dive in" / "Without further ado"
- "Game-changer" / "Cutting-edge" / "Synergy" / "Holistic" / "Paradigm"
- "Leverage" (as a verb) / "Unlock" (as a metaphor)
- "Landscape" / "Navigate" / "Elevate" / "Foster" / "Robust" / "Seamless" / "Ecosystem"
- "I wanted to reach out" / "I hope this finds you well"
- "Just circling back" / "Per our conversation" / "As per my last email"
- "Excited to announce" / "Thrilled to share"
- "Moving forward" / "Going forward" (say "from here" or just state the next step)
- "Best practices" / "North star" / "Low-hanging fruit"
- Watery qualifiers: "somewhat", "relatively", "fairly", "quite", "rather"

### Structural Patterns to Kill

- **Filler transitions.** "Moreover", "Furthermore", "Additionally", "In addition." Just start the next sentence.
- **Hedge stacking.** "It might potentially be somewhat useful." Commit to a position.
- **Sycophantic openers.** "Great question!", "That's a fantastic point!" Delete entirely.
- **Corporate throat-clearing.** Opening paragraphs that say nothing. Cut to the substance.
- **Bullet point addiction.** Convert to flowing prose unless the content genuinely needs a list.
- **Bolding everything.** Bold is for emphasis, not decoration. Max 1-2 bold phrases per section.
- **Exclamation inflation.** One exclamation mark per piece maximum. Earn it.
- **Mirror-back summarizing.** Restating what was just said before adding new info.
- **Symmetrical paragraphs.** Vary wildly. One sentence. Then a five-sentence paragraph. Then two.
- **Conclusion restates intro.** End with a forward-looking thought or a punch, not a summary.

## AI Fingerprint Cheat Sheet

If you're using AI to draft and then cleaning it up, here's what to scan for:

| AI Tell | What It Looks Like | The Fix |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| Colon-list opener | "Here are three key takeaways:" | Weave into narrative |
| Numbered listicle voice | "1. First, we need to..." | Prose paragraphs |
| Passive hedging | "It could be argued that..." | "I'd argue..." or just state it |
| Empty intensifiers | "Truly remarkable", "Incredibly powerful" | Replace with specific details |
| Uniform paragraph length | Every paragraph is 3-4 sentences | Vary: 1 sentence, then 5, then 2 |
| Over-signposting | "Let's look at... Now let's turn to..." | Trust the reader to follow |
| Rhetorical question clusters | "But what does this mean? How can we?" | Pick one or make it a statement |
| Double-adjective stacking | "innovative and impactful solution" | One adjective max, or none |
| Gerund-heavy openings | "Leveraging our capabilities..." | Start with subject + verb |
| Triple structure abuse | "X, Y, and Z" repeated across paragraphs | Use once max per piece |

The fastest AI detection test: read it out loud. If it sounds weird spoken, it reads weird written. Fix it.

## Quick Reference Card

Before you publish anything, run through this:

- [ ] Does the first sentence carry the point? (No warm-up laps)
- [ ] Are there specific numbers, names, or examples? (No vague claims)
- [ ] Does sentence length vary? (Short. Then long. Then medium.)
- [ ] Is there at least one moment of personality?
- [ ] Zero banned phrases?
- [ ] Zero em dashes?
- [ ] Max one exclamation mark?
- [ ] Does the ending punch forward, not summarize backward?
- [ ] Would Maxx actually say this?

If you can check every box, ship it.
